1. Automate Generation of Knowledge Graphs

Language Technology Institute, Carnegie Mellon University, supervised by Dr. Carolyn Rosé

  • To provide teachers with a tool to navigate through the students' discourse
  • I adopted a two-abstraction level structure for user friendliness:
    1) General (theme-based, by LDA topic modeling)
    Teachers can grasp the overview of topics in the discussion
      i) what are the topics and their frequency
      ii) where are the topics are mentioned
    2) Fine-grained (entity extraction, by Abstract Meaning Representation)
    Teachers can nagivate through the extracted subject-relation-object in detail
      i) the roles of each entity
      ii) the causality between entities

2. Foster Identity Reflection in Techno-Social Robots

Learning Research & Development Center, supervised by Dr. Erin Walker

  • To foster identity reflection among girls of color through dialog system design
  • I developed functionalities of a techno-social robot, Symmi, such that the learners can change its appearance by programming
  • Found that the current system fell short in fostering identity, I proposed a systematic coding scheme for what are the ways that learners reflect on their identities
  • Upon the coding scheme, I hypothesized a new dialog design in Symmi's dialog system to foster identity reflection

3. Analyze Movements of Soft Robotics

Industrial Engineering Department (Pitt), supervised by Dr. Ravi Shankar

  • To analyze and predict the behaviors of soft robotics that can self-assemble under various temperatures to be used in environmental monitoring and sampling
  • I recorded the moments of behavioral change and the parameters in lab videos
  • My initial try of the Mathematical Ring Theory to model the failed because the robotics' shapes were too random
  • I later sought for computational models such as clustering by similarities and neural networks to group the behaviors into parameter patterns such that their shapes can be better controlled

4. Animations and Analogies in Accessible CS Education

Computer Science Department (Pitt), supervised by Dr. Wonsun Ahn, Luís Oliveira, and Sherif Khattab

  • To make difficult CS concepts more accessible to diverse students by combining animations and real-world analogies
  • I developed the animation tool and helped to shape the original analogy of a dry-cleaning shop around Buffer Overflow
  • I designed and conducted user studies among K-12 and undergraduate students
  • Scratch animation poses multiple challenges, so I
      i) built state machines to monitor each scenario of results of Buffer Overflow
      ii) used dummy variables to doubly guard the trigger of "if" conditions in case some code co-occured chaotically or do not execute
